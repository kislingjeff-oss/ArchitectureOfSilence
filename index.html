What Did Not Reach Us

"Ignorance is not merely a lack of information; it is a motivated construction, a structural filter that determines what is allowed into the public consciousness."

Introduction: The Void of Knowledge

This application explores the deliberate and accidental mechanisms that have kept non-Native populations unaware of the realities of settler colonialism and the Indian Residential School (IRS) system. We often ask why we "don't know," but we rarely examine the architecture of the silence itself.

By interacting with the sections below, you will explore how education, media, and institutional "gatekeeping" act as filters. This isn't just about missing facts; it's about an active epistemology of ignorance—a way of not-knowing that is required to maintain the status quo of settler society.

Case Study in Epistemic Silence

Quakers & "What Did Not Reach Us"

How does a historically peace-centered community experience a collective amnesia? Using the writings of Quaker activist Jeff Kisling (author of Active Objection) and the work of the program Toward Right Relationship with Native Peoples (TRR), we can examine how "what did not reach us" transforms from a personal realization into an institutional movement of repair.

The Scattergood Silence (Jeff Kisling)

In his essays like Scattergood and the Hub, Kisling explores "what did not reach us" as a definitive structural condition of his youth. Attending Scattergood Friends School in the late 1960s, he experienced a total void: the school’s curriculum was completely silent on the fact that Quakers historically operated over 30 Indian boarding schools designed for forced cultural assimilation.

"What did not reach us is a statement, not a question. It is the realization of what was systematically withheld from our generation's consciousness." — Jeff Kisling

The "Benevolent" Filter

What did reach Quaker families was a highly sanitized myth of pure benevolence—stories of ancestors who simply "went to help the Indian children." This filter kept the violent mechanics of Grant’s Peace Policy and child separation completely hidden from descendants, protecting the white Quaker identity of absolute pacifism.

The Integration: TRR as the Truth Bridge

Friends Peace Teams' program, Toward Right Relationship with Native Peoples (TRR), co-directed by Paula Palmer and Jerilyn DeCoteau, acts as the direct counter-mechanism to "what did not reach us." Its primary methodology is truth-telling as a prerequisite for healing.

  • Unearthing Archives: TRR researches and publishes the raw evidence of Quaker involvement in boarding schools, pulling records out of local meeting vaults.
  • "Roots of Injustice" Workshops: Relational workshops designed to bypass intellectual defensiveness, helping modern settlers emotionally experience the historical timeline.
  • Decolonial Advocacy: Turning the grief of what did not reach us into support for the federal Truth and Healing Commission.

4. Why It Didn't Reach Us

Ignorance is maintained through multiple redundant systems. If one filter fails, another typically catches the information to prevent a systemic "unsettling."

01

Curriculum Erasure

History textbooks historically presented "pioneer" narratives where Indigenous people were either absent or relegated to a primitive past, creating a temporal distance that makes modern settler-colonialism invisible.

02

The "Peacemaker" Myth

National identity (especially in Canada) is built on being "kinder" than other colonial powers. Information about the IRS ruptures this identity, so it is psychologically rejected or "not heard."

03

Linguistic Sanitization

Using terms like "schools" instead of "internment camps" or "assimilation" instead of "cultural genocide" changes how the information is processed by the brain, reducing its perceived urgency.

04

Media Marginalization

For decades, Indigenous voices were excluded from mainstream newsrooms. When survivors spoke out, they were framed as "fringe" or "unreliable," ensuring their testimonies didn't reach the broader public.

Piercing the Silence

Understanding "what did not reach us" is the first step toward dismantling settler ignorance. It requires an active commitment to seeking the truths that have been filtered out and recognizing the structural reasons for our own unknowing.
